Week 3: Choosing Joints & Understanding Radiata Pine

Introduction

This week, you’ll start making decisions that shape how your Carry-all will look and hold together. The biggest one? Which joint to use for the corners. You’ve got two excellent options — the dovetail joint or the box-pin (finger) joint. Both are strong, both are used by professional woodworkers, and both will test your skills.

You’ll also learn more about Radiata Pine, the timber you’ll be working with. It might be easy to cut, but it also has challenges — and knowing the material is just as important as knowing your tools.

Comparing Joints: Dovetail vs Box-Pin

The corner joint is where your Carry-all gets most of its strength. Here's a quick breakdown of your two choices:

Dovetail Joint

  • Has angled “tails” and “pins” that lock together
  • Known for mechanical strength even without glue
  • Requires very accurate marking and careful hand cutting
  • Recognised as a sign of high craftsmanship
  • Great for traditional-looking projects

Box-Pin (Finger) Joint

  • Uses square “fingers” that interlock like puzzle pieces
  • Relies on glue for strength — lots of surface area means a very strong bond
  • Can be cut by hand or with jigs/templates
  • Modern, clean look that suits practical projects
  • Easier to lay out than dovetails

Tip: The choice isn’t just about appearance — it’s also about your comfort with marking and cutting accurately. Dovetails are harder to fix if you make a mistake.

Radiata Pine: What You’re Working With

Radiata Pine is a plantation-grown softwood used all over Australia in furniture, framing, and student projects. It’s a great timber to learn with because it’s:

  • Soft and easy to shape with hand tools
  • Uniform in density, which means it cuts cleanly
  • Stable, as long as it’s selected carefully

But it’s not perfect:

  • Knots can be hard and unpredictable when sawing
  • It dents easily, so you’ll need to handle it carefully
  • It’s not suitable for outdoors unless treated

Marking Out for Joinery

Before you cut either joint, marking out is critical. You’ll keep using the face side and face edge system and introduce some new tools:

  • Marking gauge to scribe consistent baselines
  • Try square for right angles
  • Marking knife for ultra-precise layout lines

Important rule: Always work from your reference edge and side. Never measure from a random face, or your joint will be out of alignment.
